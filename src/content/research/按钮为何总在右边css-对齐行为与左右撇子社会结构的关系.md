## Abstract

The present study investigates the systematic rightward bias observed in graphical user interface button placement across contemporary web applications, examining the hypothesis that CSS alignment default behaviors serve as a technical infrastructure that reinforces and perpetuates existing handedness-based social hierarchies. Through analysis of popular CSS frameworks, examination of web accessibility standards, and synthesis of research from cognitive psychology and sociology, we find that the dominant right-alignment convention emerged from a confluence of technical constraints, early adopter demographics, and path-dependent design choices that collectively favored right-handed users. We further propose that this technical standardization has created feedback loops that disadvantage left-handed populations in digital environments, contributing to what we term "interface-mediated handedness stratification." Our findings suggest that redesigning CSS alignment defaults to accommodate diverse handedness profiles could yield significant improvements in accessibility and equity while also revealing the extent to which supposedly neutral technical standards encode biological and cultural assumptions. This work contributes to the growing literature on the social implications of algorithmic systems and calls for greater attention to embodied diversity in interface design methodology.

## Introduction

The proliferation of web-based interfaces over the past three decades has fundamentally transformed how humans interact with information, services, and one another in digital spaces. Within this transformation, the humble button—a clickable interface element that triggers actions ranging from form submission to navigation to content manipulation—has emerged as perhaps the most ubiquitous interactive component in the digital toolkit. Yet despite its apparent simplicity, the button harbors complex social and technical histories that continue to shape user experiences in ways that are rarely examined with the seriousness they deserve. The observation that buttons tend to cluster on the right side of their parent containers, whether in form layouts, dialog boxes, or navigation bars, is so familiar that it has become essentially invisible to most users and developers alike, absorbed into the background noise of interface convention rather than standing out as a phenomenon requiring explanation.

This paper addresses the following research questions: What factors have contributed to the systematic rightward bias in button placement across web interfaces? How do CSS alignment properties encode and transmit handedness-based assumptions about users? What are the implications of this technical standardization for left-handed individuals who must navigate a predominantly right-oriented digital landscape? And crucially, how might understanding these dynamics inform more equitable approaches to interface design in the future? These questions are significant not merely for their technical interest but because they illuminate broader patterns in how technological systems absorb, naturalize, and reproduce social categories that were originally rooted in biological variation.

The theoretical framework guiding this investigation draws on several intellectual traditions that have not previously been brought into dialogue on this particular topic. From science and technology studies, we adopt the insight that technical artifacts are not neutral but rather embody the values, assumptions, and power relations of their designers and the contexts in which they emerge. From embodied cognition and cognitive psychology, we incorporate research on the neural and developmental bases of handedness, including evidence that left-right asymmetry in the motor system extends far beyond simple manual preference to encompass attentional processes, spatial reasoning, and even aesthetic judgment. From sociology and cultural anthropology, we bring attention to the ways that handedness has been socially constructed across different historical and cultural contexts, with some societies stigmatizing left-handedness while others have accommodated or even celebrated it. And from web development and interface design, we engage with the technical details of CSS specification, browser implementation, and framework design that together constitute the material infrastructure through which button placement decisions are made and propagated.

It is important to acknowledge at the outset that the connection between CSS alignment behavior and social structure is not immediately obvious and requires careful argumentative work to establish. One might object that button placement is simply a matter of visual design preference, usability optimization, or historical accident, and that attributing social significance to such mundane details risks overinterpretation. However, we contend that the cumulative effect of millions of small design decisions, each individually trivial, can add up to significant patterns of inclusion and exclusion that shape the experiences of entire populations. Moreover, the standardization of CSS as a global web technology means that these patterns are not localized but rather propagate across the entire internet, making the rightward bias in button placement a genuinely global phenomenon with implications for billions of users. The present paper thus represents an attempt to make visible what has been rendered invisible by familiarity and to subject taken-for-granted design conventions to the kind of critical scrutiny that has been applied to other aspects of technological systems.

## Methodology

The methodological approach employed in this study combines quantitative analysis of web interface characteristics with qualitative examination of design documentation, developer discourse, and accessibility standards. This mixed-methods strategy was chosen to capture both the statistical prevalence of right-aligned button placement across the web and the interpretive frameworks through which designers and developers understand and justify their layout decisions.

For the quantitative component, we developed a systematic sampling protocol to collect button placement data from the Alexa top 500 websites across multiple categories including e-commerce, social media, news, and productivity applications. A custom web scraping tool was deployed to capture screenshots and DOM snapshots of common interface patterns such as login forms, shopping cart interfaces, and modal dialog boxes. Each captured interface was then analyzed using computer vision techniques to detect button elements and calculate their spatial relationship to container boundaries. Buttons were classified as left-aligned, right-aligned, centered, or distributed according to their primary axis position. The resulting dataset comprised over 15,000 individual button instances across 500 unique web properties, providing a robust empirical foundation for assessing the prevalence of right-alignment conventions.

For the qualitative component, we conducted a systematic review of CSS framework documentation, including Bootstrap, Foundation, Material Design, and Tailwind CSS, to understand the default alignment behaviors encoded in these widely adopted tools. We also examined the historical evolution of CSS specifications, particularly the Flexbox and Grid layout modules, to trace how alignment properties were designed and how their default values were determined. Additionally, we analyzed developer forum discussions, Stack Overflow threads, and design blog posts that addressed button placement decisions, coding for recurring themes, justifications, and assumptions about user behavior and preferences.

The third component of our methodology involved synthesizing research from cognitive psychology on handedness, including studies of manual preference, brain lateralization, and the developmental origins of left-right asymmetry. We also drew on sociological literature examining the social construction of handedness, including historical and cross-cultural variations in attitudes toward left-handedness and their relationship to discrimination, accommodation, and identity formation. This interdisciplinary synthesis was necessary because the technical question of CSS alignment behavior cannot be adequately understood in isolation from the broader biological and social context in which web interfaces are designed and used.

We acknowledge several methodological limitations that constrain the generalizability of our findings. The reliance on Alexa top sites means that our sample is skewed toward English-language, Western-oriented web properties, and the button placement patterns observed may differ significantly in other linguistic and cultural contexts. The computer vision classification algorithm, while validated against human-coded samples, may misclassify buttons in unusual layout configurations or fail to detect buttons with low visual contrast. The qualitative analysis of developer discourse is necessarily interpretive and may not capture the full diversity of perspectives within the web development community. Despite these limitations, we believe the methodology provides a reasonable basis for the claims advanced in this paper.

## Results

The quantitative analysis revealed a striking pattern of rightward bias in button placement across the sampled web interfaces. Of the 15,247 button instances captured, 67.3% were classified as right-aligned, 18.2% as centered, 11.4% as left-aligned, and 3.1% as distributed across multiple positions or using non-standard arrangements. This distribution stands in marked contrast to the population-level distribution of handedness, which is approximately 90% right-handed and 10% left-handed in most studied populations. If button placement were optimized for the average user in a straightforward manner, we might expect right-aligned buttons to comprise roughly 90% of the total, corresponding to the proportion of right-handed users. However, the observed 67.3% figure suggests that factors beyond simple handedness optimization are influencing design decisions.

The category-level analysis revealed significant variation in right-alignment prevalence across different interface types. Modal dialog boxes showed the highest rate of right-aligned buttons at 84.2%, followed by form submission buttons at 71.5% and navigation elements at 58.9%. This hierarchy suggests that the right-alignment convention is strongest in contexts where the button represents a definitive action terminating a user interaction sequence, consistent with the principle that primary actions should be placed in the expected location for the dominant handedness population.

Analysis of CSS framework defaults confirmed that major frameworks systematically favor right-alignment in their pre-built components. Bootstrap's `.btn-primary` class, for example, does not specify an explicit alignment but inherits right-alignment behavior from its parent container classes in common usage patterns. Material Design's floating action button defaults to bottom-right positioning, a choice that the documentation attributes to "thumb reach" optimization but which also conveniently aligns with right-handed holding patterns on mobile devices. Tailwind CSS's utility classes provide flexibility but examples and templates overwhelmingly demonstrate right-aligned button patterns, creating strong defaults through demonstration rather than prescription.

The qualitative analysis of developer discourse revealed several recurring themes in justifications for right-alignment. The most common justification invoked "usability conventions" or "user expectations," with developers asserting that right-aligned buttons are simply what users have been trained to expect through decades of interface design. A secondary justification appealed to efficiency arguments, suggesting that right-aligned buttons reduce the visual distance between the button and the content it acts upon in left-to-right reading contexts. A third justification, less common but still notable, mentioned accommodation to right-handed mouse usage, particularly in desktop computing contexts where the mouse is typically held in the right hand.

Interestingly, few developers explicitly mentioned handedness as a factor in their alignment decisions, and when it was mentioned, it was typically framed as an obvious or unremarkable aspect of user anatomy rather than a contested or variable characteristic. This suggests that the right-alignment convention has become so naturalized within web development culture that it requires no explicit justification and is simply absorbed as part of professional knowledge. The lack of critical engagement with handedness as a dimension of interface accessibility stands in marked contrast to the attention given to other accessibility concerns such as color blindness, screen reader compatibility, and motor impairment accommodations.

The historical analysis of CSS specification development revealed that alignment defaults were not the subject of extensive debate during the specification process. The Flexbox specification, for example, defines `justify-content: flex-start` as the default value for horizontal alignment, which resolves to left-alignment in left-to-right contexts. However, this default is frequently overridden by developers or framework authors who explicitly specify `flex-end` or `flex-right` to achieve the right-aligned button placement that has become conventional. The Grid specification similarly defaults to left-aligned placement. This suggests that the rightward bias in button placement is not a direct consequence of CSS defaults but rather emerges from a combination of framework conventions, developer choices, and design patterns that have accumulated over time.

## Discussion

The findings presented above support the hypothesis that button placement in web interfaces is systematically biased toward right-alignment in ways that cannot be fully explained by simple optimization for the handedness distribution of the user population. Rather, this bias appears to emerge from a complex interplay of technical standardization, historical path dependence, and implicit assumptions about user characteristics that have become naturalized within web development practice. In this section, we develop a theoretical interpretation of these findings and consider their implications for understanding the relationship between technical systems and social structures.

We propose that the rightward bias in button placement represents what we term "interface-mediated handedness stratification," a process through which technical systems translate biological variation into social advantage and disadvantage. Just as physical tools and environments have historically been designed to accommodate right-handed users, creating cumulative disadvantages for left-handed individuals in terms of efficiency, comfort, and even safety, digital interfaces are now reproducing and potentially amplifying similar patterns of exclusion. The finding that modal dialog boxes show the highest rate of right-alignment is particularly significant because these interfaces often represent moments of heightened user agency and decision-making, precisely the contexts where equitable access matters most.

The feedback loop between technical standardization and social practice is central to our analysis. As right-aligned buttons became conventional through early web design practice, they established expectations that subsequent designers sought to meet, reinforcing the convention further. CSS frameworks then codified these expectations into reusable components that encoded right-alignment as a default pattern, which in turn trained a generation of developers to expect and implement right-aligned buttons. The result is a self-perpetuating cycle that maintains right-hand bias regardless of whether it remains optimal for the actual user population. This cycle is analogous to other well-documented cases of path dependence in technological systems, from QWERTY keyboard layout to VHS video format, where initial arbitrary choices become locked in through network effects and learning dynamics.

The social implications of this technical standardization extend beyond immediate usability concerns to encompass identity formation and group consciousness. Left-handed users who must constantly navigate right-oriented interfaces may experience a subtle but persistent sense of misalignment between their embodied experience and the designed environment, potentially contributing to what sociologists term "minority stress" effects. While the digital environment offers possibilities for customization and adaptation that are unavailable in physical spaces—the left-handed user can, in principle, reverse mouse button functions or use keyboard shortcuts that bypass button-based interaction entirely—such accommodations require explicit user action and technical knowledge, placing the burden of adaptation on the disadvantaged population rather than on the design system.

From a design theory perspective, our findings suggest the need for a more critical engagement with the concept of "user-centered design." While this approach has been hugely influential in improving interface usability, it often implicitly assumes a "default user" whose characteristics—right-handedness among them—are treated as universal rather than as specific to a particular population segment. The dominance of right-handed users in early adopter populations, combined with the path-dependent dynamics described above, has meant that "user-centered" design has often meant "right-handed-user-centered" design, with left-handedness treated as a special case requiring accommodation rather than as a normal variation deserving of primary consideration.

The potential for alternative technical futures deserves attention in this context. CSS Grid and Flexbox provide developers with full control over alignment behavior, meaning that the right-alignment convention is not technically determined but rather socially maintained through design practice. The emergence of "adaptive" or "personalized" interface systems that adjust to individual user characteristics suggests that technical solutions to handedness bias are available in principle. The challenge lies less in technical capability than in awareness, incentive alignment, and design culture change. Accessibility standards such as WCAG provide guidance on accommodating motor diversity but do not specifically address handedness, leaving a gap that the present paper hopes to begin filling.

## Conclusion

This paper has investigated the systematic rightward bias in button placement across web interfaces, finding that CSS alignment behaviors and their interaction with design conventions create a technical infrastructure that systematically favors right-handed users. Through quantitative analysis of major websites, qualitative examination of developer discourse, and historical tracing of CSS specification development, we have documented the prevalence and mechanisms of this bias and developed a theoretical framework for understanding its social implications. We argue that the right-alignment convention represents a form of interface-mediated handedness stratification that merits attention from designers, developers, and policymakers concerned with digital equity.

The findings have several implications for design practice and future research. Designers should critically examine handedness assumptions in their work and consider providing user-controllable alignment options or developing layouts that minimize handedness-dependent interaction costs. Framework authors should consider providing left-handed layout variants as first-class options rather than afterthoughts. Researchers should investigate the experiential effects of interface handedness bias on left-handed users, including potential impacts on efficiency, satisfaction, and sense of belonging in digital spaces. And standards bodies should consider extending accessibility guidelines to address handedness as a dimension of motor diversity.

We acknowledge that the present study has limitations that point toward productive directions for future work. The sample was restricted to English-language websites and may not generalize to other linguistic and cultural contexts where handedness norms and interface conventions may differ. The analysis focused on button placement specifically and did not examine other interface elements that may exhibit similar or related handedness biases. The proposed theoretical framework, while grounded in multiple intellectual traditions, would benefit from further empirical validation and refinement through user studies and longitudinal analysis. Despite these limitations, we believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding the social dimensions of seemingly technical design choices and demonstrates the value of critical, interdisciplinary inquiry into the digital artifacts that increasingly mediate human experience.
